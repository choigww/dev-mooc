Priscus of Epirus

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
Jump to: navigation, search

Priscus of Epirus (c. 305 – c. 395 AD) was a Neoplatonist philosopher and theurgist, a colleague of Maximus of Ephesus, and a friend of the emperor Julian.

Priscus was a pupil of Aedesius in Pergamon, and later went to teach in Athens, where he taught Julian.[1] When Julian was in Gaul, he wrote to Priscus in the hope of acquiring the writings of Iamblichus on the Chaldean Oracles.[2] When Julian was proclaimed Caesar he summoned Priscus to Gaul, and he took him with him to Constantinople when he became Augustus in 361.[3] Priscus and Maximus travelled with Julian on campaign in Persia, and they were with him when he died in 363.[4] Sometime after the death of Julian, Priscus was arrested but eventually freed, avoiding the fate of Maximus who was executed in 371.[5] Priscus returned to Athens where he continued to teach for more than thirty years.[6]
